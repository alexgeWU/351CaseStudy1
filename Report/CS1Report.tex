\section{Background}

Audio recordings often contain unwanted noise that can degrade the quality of the sound. 
The ability to selectively amplify or attenuate specific frequency bands is fundamental to modern audio processing systems, 
including equalizers found in smartphones, music players, and professional audio equipment.
By modifying the spectral content of a signal, equalization can enhance clarity, tonal balance, and suppress unwanted noise.

A multi-band equalizer is a powerful tool for enhancing audio by allowing users to adjust the amplitude of specific frequency bands.
This is accomplished by decomposing an input signal into several frequency-selective components using filters
such as low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, and band-stop filters. 
Each band is independently scaled by a gain factor before recombining the filtered signals.
This structure enables targeted control over bass, midrange, and treble content while preserving overall signal integrity.

Frequency-selective filtering can be implemented using continuous-time circuit models, which provide well-characterized impulse and frequency responses. 
First-order RC filters allow straightforward analytical modeling of magnitude and phase behavior, making them suitable for constructing modular equalizer stages. 
When multiple filters operate in parallel and are combined through weighted summation, the resulting system achieves customizable spectral shaping.

Equalization additionally plays a critical role in environmental audio analysis.
Natural recordings, such as bird vocalizations, often include wind noise and broadband interference that 
obscure important biologically relevant features. Spectrogram analysis, the visualization of time-varying 
frequency content, enables identification of recurring vocalization patterns.
Through appropriate frequency bands and gain settings, an equalizer can isolate and amplify bird calls while reducing background noise,
facilitating more accurate species identification and behavioral studies.

This project focuses on modeling and designing a continuous-time multi-band equalizer to enhance music recordings and improve the clarity and 
identification of bird vocalizations in noisy environments through spectral analysis.

\subsection{Formulation}
Let $h_i(t)$ be the impulse response of an linear time-invariant (LTI) system that is designed to pick out a specific band of frequencies. Figure 1 shows five of these systems that pick out a band of frequency. Note that the frequency ranges are obtained as a result of parameters discussed later in the design.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

In order to implement $h_i(t)$, we use a band-pass filter. Resistor-inductor-capacitor (RLC) circuits are networks that have long been used as a method to implement band-pass filters. We can derive transfer functions using the impedance of the devices, and because these devices are linear, they can be applied to each sinusoidal component of an input function. Consider the following circuit:

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\begin{circuitikz}

      to[L, l=$L$] (3,2)
      to[C, l=$C$] (5,2)
      to[R, l=$R$] (5,0)
      -- (6,0)
      -- (0,0);

\draw (5,2) -- (6,2);

% terminal dots

\draw (6,0) node[circ]{};

% polarity labels
\node at (6.3,2) {$+$};
\node at (6.3,0) {$-$};

% output label
\node at (6.4,1) {$v_{out}(t)$};

\end{circuitikz}
\caption{Series RLC band-pass filter with output measured across the resistor.}
\end{figure}

This circuit picks out one frequency band and attenuates others based on the values passed into $R$, $L$, and $C$. Equation 1 is the $i$'th's circuit's transfer function where $i =1,2, ..., 5$ (see Appendix II.A for derivation).

\begin{equation}
\large
     X_i(jw) = \frac{jw\frac{R}{L}}{(j\omega)^2 + j\omega \frac{R}{L} + \frac{1}{L C}}
\end{equation}

Looking at Equation 1, we can see that if $\omega =0$, $X_i(j\omega) = 0$, and if $\omega \to \infty$, $X_i(j\omega) \to 0$. Thus we know that this filter is neither lowpass nor highpass.

Next, we will introduce the continuous time linear constant coefficient differential equation (LCCDE) for this circuit, which is given by (see Appendix II.B for derivation).

\begin{equation}
    \large
    \frac{dv_{in}}{dt} = \frac{L}{R}\frac{d^2v_{out}}{dt^2} + \frac{dv_{out}}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC}v_{out} 
\end{equation}

Different circuit parameters were chosen for each task. This is because in each task, we want to pick different bands, and within those bands, sometimes we may need to limit the bandwidth. We controlled the center frequency by using the fact that $\omega_c=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$. In order to control the bandwidth, we utilized the idea of the Q-factor of the circuit, given by
\begin{equation}
    \large
    Q = \frac{w_cL}{R}
\end{equation}

Intuitively, the $Q$ factor measures how well the circuit can "pick" out its frequency range. For a higher Q-factor, the bandwidth is smaller, and vice versa. Because $Q \sim R^{-1}$, we can increase the bandwidth by increasing $R$, and vice versa. 

The optimal input parameters $R$, $L$, and $C$ for each circuit and each task were determined qualitatively through trial and error and are listed in Appendix II.C.

\begin{equation}

    V_{out} = V_{in}\frac{R}{R + j\omega L - \frac{j}{\omega C}}

\begin{equation}

     V_{out} = V_{in}\frac{jw\frac{R}{L}}{(j\omega)^2 + j\omega \frac{R}{L} + \frac{1}{L C}}

\begin{equation}

\subsection{Circuit Continuous Time LCCDE Derivation}
From the circuit in Figure x, perform Kirchhoff's Voltage Law.

\begin{equation}
    \large
    v_{in}(t)= L\frac{di}{dt} +  \frac{1}{C}\int i(t) dt +  iR
\end{equation}

Now, differentiate both sides.
\begin{equation}
    \large
    \frac{dv_{in}}{dt} = L\frac{d^2i}{dt^2} + R\frac{di}{dt} + \frac{1}{C}i 
\end{equation}

We know that $v_{out}=iR \implies i = \frac{v_{out}}{R}$. So,

\begin{equation}
    \large
    \frac{dv_{in}}{dt} = \frac{L}{R}\frac{d^2v_{out}}{dt^2} + \frac{dv_{out}}{dt} + \frac{1}{RC}v_{out} 
\end{equation}
